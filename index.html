About Me

I'm a Ph.D. graduate and geospatial data scientist with an interest in unraveling the intricacies of complex datasets. My expertise lies in applying machine learning algorithms and data analytics, particularly within geospatial data such as SAR/optical satellite imagery, to assess environmental systems. Currently, I plan to use my experience in research and statistical modeling to contribute to impactful and diverse projects.

I received a Ph.D. in geology at the University of California, Los Angeles in 2023 under the supervision of Prof. Seulgi Moon. My research involved determining highly susceptible areas to landsliding using machine learning algorithms as well as understanding the environmental conditions that likely contribute to landslide initiation. Additionally, I sought to better understand and quantify erosion and tectonic uplift in the easternmost Himalaya over varying timescales, which together govern the landscape evolution of the region.

Ph.D. Research

My Ph.D. research was centered around three topics related to the landscape evolution of the easternmost Himalayas in the Indian state of Arunachal Pradesh over decadal, millennial, and million-year timescales. I aimed to better understand present-day natural hazards to long-term erosion rates and patterns that have wide-reaching implications for public safety and shaping our world. In my research, I quantified:

  1. Landslide susceptibility using machine learning and statistical modeling
  2. Landslide-derived decadal and 10Be-derived millennial erosion rates
  3. Million-year timescale exhumation rates
in the easternmost Himalaya to better understand how climate, topography, and tectonic activity influence surface processes and erosion over different timescales.

Landslide susceptibility

modeling

Landslides directly cause large economic losses and numerous deaths globally each year with lasting secondary effects such as perturbations to ecosystems through damming and flooding. With the rapid onset of climate change, the frequency of rainfall-induced landslides is only expected to increase, further endangering public safety especially for remote communities with limited resources.

However, landslides are notoriously challenging to predict because a wide range of factors can influence the stability of a hillslope. Numerous physical, statistical, and machine learning models have been applied to accurately estimate the probability that a landslide may occur in a specific location, but each type of model suffers from its own shortcomings. These shortcomings include accounting for a limited amount of triggering factors, accuracy, and interpretability. Thus, we introduce the superposable neural network (SNN), which incorporates high performance and interpretability.

The SNN model found that a combination of hillslope gradient and precipitation as well as slope face direction are primary contributors to high landslide susceptibility in this region. Our result supports the importance of subsurface soil saturation for landslide occurrences in the easternmost Himalayas. Additionally, we created an innovative approach to determine local, primary triggering factors for landslides within smaller 2.25 km2 areas. The SNN outperformed commonly used statistical models including logistic regression and likelihood ratios.

Our study highlights the growing abilities of machine learning algorithms to inform researchers about physical mechanisms driving natural hazards. Please refer to our publication in Communications Earth & Environment, article on UCLA Newsroom, or short segments on The Jefferson Exchange and Spectrum News for more details!

Erosion Rate Controls

In everyday life, erosion or the transport of sediment is commonly associated with water, possibly as rivers carving through mountain faces and depositing material downstream. However, geomorphologists traditionally believe that hillslope erosion is controlled by tectonic activity, which dictates the amount of rock material available for erosion while water and precipitation merely shape the land surface and elevation.

Although many studies have demonstrated correlations between tectonic activity, or related landscape features such as topography, and erosion rate, other theoretical studies have determined that climate-driven erosional efficiency could potentially influence mountain formation. This link implies that an appropriately large climate forcing may raise the efficiency of a landscape to erode to a degree that could trigger a tectonic response. However, observing an elevated erosional efficiency due to climate through field studies is difficult. Nevertheless, unraveling the controls of this dynamic system is crucial for refining landscape evolution models and expanding our understanding of how the world came to be.

In this study, we hypothesized that climate’s ambiguous influence on erosional efficiency may be confounded by lithology or rock type. To test this, we measured and compiled 173 erosion rates representing millennial timescales across the Himalayas and their associated climatic, topographic, and lithologic metrics. Because hard or soft rock may decrease or increase erosional efficiency, respectively, separating by rock type allows for a clearer understanding of the effect of climate on erosion. Additionally, we explore the potential role of landslides as a climate-driven erosional mechanism using a landslide time-series inventory.

This manuscript is in review at Nature Communications.

Quantify Exhumation Rates

The Himalayas are one of the most tectonically active mountain ranges in the world being the home of 10 of the world’s 14 peaks above 8000 m in elevation (though this is also related to the Himalayas being a continental-continental convergent boundary). The 1950 Mw 8.6 Assam earthquake located at the easternmost Himalayas is the largest earthquake ever recorded on land. Additionally, horizontal strain estimates and missing rock units relative to other Himalayan regions suggest extreme tectonic and erosional activity. However, studies examining the fault structure, rate of slip, and exhumation history particularly in the easternmost range front are limited.

In this study, we measure and compile thermochronologic data along a transect from the range front to the hinterland. Inverse thermal modeling offer insights into plausible spatial distributions of exhumation and help elucidate potential links among, climate, tectonics, and erosion rates in the easternmost Himalaya.

Aerial Imagery Segmentation

Semantic segmentation is a type of image segmentation where pixels in an image are assigned to different classes. This type of digital image processing has wide reaching applications including Tesla’s self-driving algorithms, environmental monitoring, urban planning, disaster response and recovery, among countless others.

U-Net was introduced in 2015 by Ronneberger et al. as a convolutional neural network for semantic segmentation of biomedical images. At the time, U-Net’s architecture was novel in its skip connections and decoder networks to provide spatial context and up-sample feature maps, respectively, to generate a segmentation mask. This network was designed to be efficiently trained on very few images and able to perform segmentation quickly using recent consumer-grade GPUs.

In this project, I explored the use of U-Net in the segmentation of Dubai aerial imagery published by Humans in the Loop. I utilized a traditional U-Net architecture in PyTorch except for batch normalization layers between convolution and max pooling layers to address vanishing gradients. Furthermore, I investigated and compared the impact of cross entropy and focal loss functions on segmentation performance for this dataset. My preliminary findings indicated that although the network converges quicker with focal loss, within 20 epochs both achieve a similar Jaccard Index coefficient. Future efforts with a more capable GPU would include additional iterations and epochs to robustly capture model performance. Additionally, I would investigate if the model has systematic difficulty classifying any specific class, which could be limited by the dataset or model.
